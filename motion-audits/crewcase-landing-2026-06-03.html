OVERALL

The motion posture here is ambitious and almost right. The hero entrance sequence is genuinely polished — staggered, physics-calibrated, with a phone reveal that uses blur for material weight. The prefers-reduced-motion guard is present, which is the correct foundation. But two patterns undercut the effort: a uniform slide-up reveal applied to every element on scroll (killing the specificity the hero earns), and a form success state that hard-cuts between form and confirmation without any transition — a jarring gap that lands right at the trust-critical moment. Fix those two and the page goes from "has animations" to "feels considered." The stagger density across sections is a secondary concern worth tightening; five staggered lists makes the pattern feel automatic rather than deliberate.

Issues to address

Critical Jakub Emil

Uniform reveal applied to every element

WhatEvery .reveal element receives the same opacity:0 → translateY(22px) entrance. Stats, feature cards, testimonials, steps, manifest rows, section headings — all appear with an identical physical motion. At the audit threshold of 4+ instances the pattern triggers the anti-checklist's "uniform reveal reflex" flag. More than 12 distinct elements are wired to this single behavior.

Why it mattersJakub's production polish lens requires that each reveal express the material it's revealing. A stat counter entering the same way as a long-form testimonial card is a category mismatch — the motion stops communicating. The hero entrance earned trust by being specific; the scroll system gives it back.

Recommended motionDifferentiate reveals by element category. Stats: counter tick-up on entry (no translateY). Testimonial cards: 12px horizontal slide-in from left, no Y. Manifest rows: staggered left-in at 6px, faster (400ms). Headings: keep translateY but drop duration to 380ms and eliminate reveal for <p> body copy entirely. Reserve the translateY for structural reveals only (section containers, not their children).

Critical Jakub

Form → success state hard-cuts with no transition

WhatAfter a successful waitlist submission, the form is instantly hidden and the success message is instantly shown via form.style.display = 'none'; success.style.display = 'block'. No opacity crossfade, no translateY, nothing. The user's action — submitting their email — is answered with a jarring DOM swap.

Why it mattersThis is the single highest-stakes UI transition on the page. The user has just taken the primary conversion action. Jakub's lens flags unpolished interactive state changes as a production blocker — the success message should feel like a confirmation, not a glitch. The abrupt cut undermines trust precisely when trust is being built.

Recommended motionFade-and-scale out the form (opacity: 0; transform: scale(0.97); transition: 200ms ease-out), then after 200ms fade-and-slide up the success state (opacity: 0 → 1; translateY(6px → 0); 320ms cubic-bezier(0.16,1,0.3,1)). Use setTimeout to sequence. Add prefers-reduced-motion fallback that crossfades at 1ms. Total perceived duration: ~500ms — fast enough to not delay, long enough to feel intentional.

Issues to address

Important Jhey Emil

Stagger applied to 5+ separate lists — the pattern becomes wallpaper

WhatStagger delays (d1–d4) are applied across at minimum: the phone reveal row, the stats band, the testimonials, the capabilities manifest rows, and the how-it-works steps. Each list uses the same 80ms interval. At 2+ staggered lists the anti-checklist flags density concern; at 5 it's pattern saturation — the user stops registering it as a design choice and starts experiencing it as lag.

Why it mattersFrom Jhey's lens: stagger is expressive only when it's used for a specific reason. Here the reason is "these are list items" applied uniformly. The anti-checklist's stagger-spam gate triggers at ≥2 staggered lists. This instance has 5. Jhey would want at most one deliberate staggered reveal per section.

Recommended motionReserve stagger for one list per page section. The manifest rows are the best candidate — they're the densest information structure and the horizontal reading rhythm benefits from a left-to-right sequence. Remove stagger from the stats (counter tick-up is better), testimonials (simultaneous or no animation), and how-it-works steps (a single parent container entrance). Cut max stagger to 3 items max per list; d4 at 0.32s with a 650ms reveal is 970ms total delay — too long.

Issues to address

Important Emil

Headings and body copy wired to motion-on-mount

WhatThe .reveal class is applied to <h2>, <p>, and <h3> elements throughout the page — not just structural containers. Section headings, feature descriptions, and testimonial paragraphs all receive the scroll-trigger entrance. Emil's framework asks: does this element need to announce itself? Text that the user is already reading doesn't. Motion on static prose is distraction, not enhancement.

Why it mattersEmil's rule: if motion doesn't communicate something specific about the content type, it shouldn't exist. "I am a paragraph that appeared" is not information. The result is that every section of the page carries a performance overhead — IntersectionObserver callbacks, class additions, transition repaints — for effects that don't improve comprehension or signal anything meaningful.

Recommended motionRemove .reveal from all <p> elements and from heading text nodes directly. Keep it on section containers (<section>, .feat-split, .feat-pair) so the container enters, but the children are already visible within it. This halves the number of observed elements and moves motion to structural level, which is meaningful.
